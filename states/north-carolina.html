How total loss claims work in North Carolina

Three things decide what you're paid after a North Carolina total loss: the actual cash value the insurer assigns your car, the evidence you bring to challenge it, and the dispute rights under North Carolina law.

Total Loss Threshold: 75%

North Carolina uses a 75% fixed total-loss threshold: a vehicle is generally a total loss when the cost of repair reaches at least 75% of its actual cash value.

Your right to appraisal

Not mandated by state statute. Most North Carolina auto policies include an appraisal clause. The state does require insurers to provide a reasonable basis for total loss valuations.

Deadlines & Regulations

North Carolina requires insurers to acknowledge claims within a reasonable time. The Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act sets standards for prompt investigation and settlement. Governed under N.C.G.S. §58-63-15 (Unfair Claim Settlement Practices), N.C. Admin. Code 11 NCAC 04 .0413 by the North Carolina Department of Insurance.

Official references:North Carolina Department of Insurance, After an Accident·North Carolina General Statutes §20-71.3·North Carolina Department of Insurance, Claims Handling Rule Changes

Where an insurer valuation may need a closer look

Check the comparables

Review condition adjustments

North Carolina total loss FAQs

When is a car considered a total loss in North Carolina?

In North Carolina, total loss threshold is set to 75%. North Carolina uses a 75% fixed total-loss threshold: a vehicle is generally a total loss when the cost of repair reaches at least 75% of its actual cash value.

Can I dispute a total loss settlement offer in North Carolina?

Yes. You are not required to accept the insurer's first offer. You can present your own evidence of value - comparable local listings, options and condition documentation, and an independent vehicle value report.

Do North Carolina policies include an appraisal clause?

Not mandated by state statute. Most North Carolina auto policies include an appraisal clause. The state does require insurers to provide a reasonable basis for total loss valuations.

Can I claim diminished value in North Carolina?

Available in third-party claims. North Carolina courts recognize diminished value as a recoverable damage when another driver is at fault.

How long does the insurer have to pay my claim in North Carolina?

North Carolina requires insurers to acknowledge claims within a reasonable time. The Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act sets standards for prompt investigation and settlement.
