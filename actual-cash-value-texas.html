Texas Guide · Actual Cash Value

Your whole claim rides on three letters: ACV.

Actual cash value (ACV) is what it would cost to replace your vehicle with a comparable one - same year, trim, options, mileage, and condition - in your local market immediately before the loss. It is not trade-in value and not a book estimate. In Texas, the ACV decides both whether your car is totaled and what the insurer pays, which makes a low ACV the most expensive number to leave unchecked.

What does actual cash value mean?

When an insurer totals your car, your policy pays the vehicle's actual cash value: the money it would take to put you back in a comparable vehicle in your market, as it stood the moment before the loss. The exact wording comes from your policy, but in practice Texas auto total losses are settled on local replacement reality - what a car like yours, equipped like yours, in the condition yours was in, actually sells for in your area this week.

ACV vs market value, trade-in value, and book value

These terms get used interchangeably, and the confusion usually costs the owner:

  • Actual cash value - the policy's measure of loss: comparable retail replacement in your local market immediately before the loss.
  • Market value - loosely, what your car would sell for; for a total loss it should track the ACV, which is why big gaps between the offer and local listings are worth questioning.
  • Trade-in value - a wholesale number: what a dealer pays so it can resell at a profit. It runs well below retail replacement, and comparables built on trade-in or auction pricing drag an ACV down.
  • Book value - KBB, NADA, and Edmunds figures are modeled estimates from regional averages. Useful as a sanity check, binding on nobody.

The pattern to watch: every one of those alternatives is usually lower than true local replacement cost. If an offer quietly substitutes one of them for the ACV, you pay the difference.

How do insurance companies calculate ACV?

Texas insurers typically buy the number from valuation software - CCC ONE, Mitchell, or Audatex. The software pulls comparable listings, adjusts them for mileage and equipment, and applies "condition" adjustments to your vehicle, usually without anyone inspecting it. The result looks precise: a report full of VINs, adjustments, and decimal points. Precision isn't accuracy. The common failure points are base trims matched against loaded vehicles, comparables from distant or cheaper markets, distressed or aged listings, and condition markdowns nobody can explain.

Why the ACV controls everything in a Texas total loss

Texas has the highest total loss threshold in the nation: under Transportation Code Chapter 501, a car is generally totaled when repair costs reach 100% of its actual cash value. So the ACV isn't just your payout - it decides whether the car is totaled at all. Set it too low and a repairable car can be wrongly totaled, or a genuine total can be shortchanged. Taxes ride on it too: Texas motor vehicle sales tax is 6.25% of the value, so a low ACV also shrinks the tax and fee line items in your settlement.

How do you dispute a low ACV?

  1. Ask the adjuster, in writing, for the complete valuation report - not just the offer letter.
  2. Check every comparable against what's actually for sale in your market. Could it really replace your car this week?
  3. Gather your evidence: window sticker or build sheet, maintenance records, pre-loss photos, current local listings.
  4. Get an independent vehicle value report that rebuilds the value from verified local comparables - our total-loss appraisal packages start at $99 with 48-hour standard delivery.
  5. Counter in writing, and if the insurer won't correct the number, invoke the appraisal clause - mandatory in Texas personal auto policies issued or renewed on or after January 1, 2026 under SB 458.

Common questions

Actual cash value FAQs

Is actual cash value the same as Kelley Blue Book value?

No. Book values from KBB, NADA, or Edmunds are broad estimates built from regional averages; your policy pays actual cash value, which insurers set from specific comparable vehicles. Book values are useful as a sanity check - if the offer sits well below every major guide, that's a red flag - but they don't bind either side.

Does actual cash value include sales tax in Texas?

Replacing a car in Texas means paying 6.25% motor vehicle sales tax plus title and registration fees, and depending on your policy those amounts belong in the total loss settlement conversation. They're calculated on the ACV itself, so an undervalued car also produces an undervalued tax line. Check the settlement breakdown for these line items.

Is ACV just replacement cost minus depreciation?

That formula appears in some property policies, but auto total losses in practice are settled on the local market: what a comparable vehicle - same year, trim, options, mileage, and condition - actually sells for in your area today. The exact definition comes from your policy language, which is why reading the valuation report against real local listings matters more than any formula.

Can I dispute the insurer's ACV in Texas?

Yes. Request the full valuation report, check the comparable vehicles and condition adjustments, and counter in writing with your own evidence - local listings, options documentation, maintenance records, and an independent vehicle value report. If the insurer won't correct a low number, Texas personal auto policies issued or renewed on or after January 1, 2026 must include an appraisal clause you can invoke.

What evidence actually raises an ACV?

Evidence the software missed: the window sticker or build sheet proving trim and options, maintenance and repair receipts, pre-loss photos showing condition, new tires or major recent work, and current listings for genuinely comparable vehicles in your market. An independent vehicle value report packages that evidence to clear, documented standards.
