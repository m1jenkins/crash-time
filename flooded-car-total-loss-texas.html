Does insurance cover a flooded car in Texas?

Flood damage to your own vehicle is covered by comprehensive coverage - the same coverage that pays for hail - minus your deductible, and fault is irrelevant. Liability-only policies don't cover it, and homeowners and federal flood policies generally exclude vehicles. The source of the water doesn't matter: a hurricane on the coast, a rising bayou in Houston, or a flash flood across a Hill Country low-water crossing are all the same claim.

Why flooded cars are almost always totaled

Water is a different kind of loss than a crash. It gets into wiring harnesses, control modules, airbag sensors, and seat foam, and the damage keeps developing after the water recedes - corrosion and mold don't show up on day one. Repair estimates climb accordingly, and because Texas totals a vehicle when repair costs reach 100% of its actual cash value under Transportation Code Chapter 501, a car with water over the floorboards is usually a total. If water stayed below the interior, the total loss call can be wrong in either direction - one more reason to verify both the estimate and the valuation independently.

What is your flooded car worth?

The pre-flood actual cash value of your car: the trim and options it actually had, the condition it was actually in before the water, and what replacement actually costs in your metro now. Two flood-specific traps to check. First, condition: a car photographed full of mud invites a "poor condition" assumption, but the insurer owes you the value of the car as it stood before the flood - your pre-storm photos and maintenance records establish that. Second, the market: a major flood removes thousands of vehicles from local supply at once, so replacement prices typically rise just as offers go out, and software anchored to pre-storm listings understates them. Taxes and fees ride on the value too - see sales tax on a totaled car in Texas.

How insurers handle a flood catastrophe

The same way they handle a hailstorm, at larger scale: catastrophe teams, bulk software-driven settlements, and pressure to close files fast. Hurricane Harvey put an estimated half a million flooded vehicles into the Texas claims pipeline in a matter of days, most of them in the Houston area - and when adjusters are moving that kind of volume, the claim that gets corrected is the one with documentation attached. Flood risk is a fact of life from the coast to Houston's bayous to Central Texas flash-flood country, and every report we build is handled remotely, statewide.

How to challenge a low flood total loss offer

  1. Request the full valuation report behind the offer - not just the settlement letter.
  2. Check the comparables against what's actually for sale in your metro after the storm.
  3. Gather pre-flood evidence: options list or window sticker, maintenance records, photos taken before the water.
  4. Get an independent vehicle value report of the pre-flood value - our total-loss appraisal packages start at $99 with 48-hour standard delivery.
  5. Counter in writing, and if the insurer won't move, invoke the appraisal clause - mandatory in Texas personal auto policies issued or renewed on or after January 1, 2026 under SB 458.

Hail rather than water? The same playbook applies - see the Texas hail total loss guide.

Should you keep a flooded car?

Think hard before retaining a flood total. The car gets a flood-branded salvage title, the salvage value comes out of your settlement, and water damage has a long tail: electrical gremlins, corrosion, and mold routinely surface months later. Owner retention can make sense for a specialty vehicle or a mechanic's project; for a daily driver, the full settlement is usually the better trade - once the settlement number is actually right.

Deadlines still apply - both ways

Catastrophe backlogs don't suspend the Texas Prompt Payment of Claims Act (Insurance Code Chapter 542): insurers still face claim-handling deadlines, with 18% statutory interest when they're missed. Your clock runs too - Texas claims are generally subject to a two-year limitations period from the date of loss. Document early; don't wait for the backlog to clear.

Common questions

Flood total loss FAQs

Is flood damage covered by car insurance in Texas?

Only if you carry comprehensive coverage. Comprehensive pays for water damage from floods, hurricanes, and rising water, minus your deductible, and fault is irrelevant. Liability-only policies don't cover flood damage to your own car, and homeowners and federal flood insurance generally exclude vehicles.

Will my flooded car definitely be totaled?

Usually, if water reached the interior. Flood damage attacks electronics, wiring, and airbag systems, and corrosion and mold keep developing after the water recedes, so repair estimates climb past the Texas 100% threshold quickly. Brief water exposure below the floorboards can be repairable - which is why both the repair estimate and the valuation deserve independent checking.

Can I negotiate a flood total loss offer?

Yes. After a flood event, valuations are bulk-processed by software while local replacement prices are rising, which makes generic condition assumptions and stale comparables common - and correctable. Request the valuation report, document your car's pre-flood condition and options, and counter in writing.

Does it matter that thousands of other cars flooded at the same time?

It matters in your favor. A major flood removes thousands of vehicles from the local market at once, so replacement prices in the affected metro typically rise just as offers go out. Software anchored to pre-storm or out-of-market listings understates what it now costs to replace your car locally - exactly the error an independent appraisal documents.

Should I keep (buy back) my flooded car?

Be cautious. An owner-retained flood total gets a flood-branded salvage title, the salvage value is deducted from your settlement, and corrosion and electrical failures often surface months later. For most daily drivers, taking the full settlement is the safer path - just make sure the settlement is right first.
