A comparable should match the car people actually shop for.

The same badge on the trunk is not enough. A useful comparable should be close to your car in configuration, age, mileage, condition, market, and timing. Differences are allowed, but they need honest adjustments.

Start with the features that change the market

Rank differences by how buyers search. Trim, body style, drivetrain, engine, transmission, model generation, and a major factory package can divide one market into several. Color or a small convenience option may matter less. The right question is not whether two cars are identical. It is whether a buyer would treat them as realistic substitutes at the prices being compared.

Use this match order

  1. Identity: VIN, year, make, model, trim, and body style.
  2. Mechanical configuration: engine, drivetrain, transmission, battery or powertrain version.
  3. Equipment: factory packages and high-value options.
  4. Use and wear: mileage, condition, title status, accident history, and commercial use when known.
  5. Market context: dealer or private seller, location, listing date, and whether the car was actually available.

A weakness near the top of the list deserves more scrutiny than a small difference near the bottom. A mileage adjustment can be reasonable. Relabeling a front-wheel-drive base model as a substitute for an all-wheel-drive premium trim is a different problem.

When an adjustment can fix the difference

Comparables rarely match on every fact. A transparent adjustment can account for a measurable difference such as mileage or equipment. Check whether it moves in the right direction and whether the report explains enough to follow the math. An adjustment becomes less convincing when it tries to bridge a different model generation, rare powertrain, branded title, prior damage, or missing market segment.

Check the listing, not just the report summary

Open the source listing. Confirm the VIN where possible. Compare the photos and description with the report. If the listing disappeared, call the dealer and ask whether the vehicle existed, whether the advertised price was accurate, whether it sold, and how it was equipped. A short note with the date, dealer name, and answer is better evidence than an unsupported claim that the comp is fake.

Score the comp before you challenge it

CheckStrong matchNeeds explanation
Trim and drivetrainSame trim and drivetrainLower trim or different driven wheels
Age and generationSame year or same generationRedesign or major equipment change
MileageClose mileage with clear adjustmentLarge gap or unexplained direction
Condition and historySimilar, documented statusPrior damage, branded title, or unclear condition
Market and dateRelevant market and current listingDistant, stale, unavailable, or unverifiable

Send a replacement, not just an objection

If a comp is weak, say why in one sentence and provide a better candidate when you can. Save the listing as a PDF, capture the date, record the VIN and dealer, and explain why it is closer to your vehicle. Three well-documented listings usually make a cleaner point than twenty loosely related search results.

Use the counteroffer template to put the comparison in writing. If the weak comps appear in a CCC report, work through the complete CCC ONE audit before you send it.
