Value the car you owned, not the base model.

A wrong trim, drivetrain, engine, or factory package can affect every comparable and adjustment that follows. Prove the configuration first. Then ask for a corrected report instead of debating a value built on the wrong vehicle.

Do not stop at a basic VIN decoder

A VIN can establish many core facts, but it does not always identify every package, standalone option, dealer-installed item, or later modification. Start with the VIN, then confirm the build with documents tied to the actual car.

  • Original window sticker or Monroney label
  • Manufacturer build sheet or dealer parts-department printout
  • Purchase contract or original dealer invoice
  • Equipment labels, owner's app records, and clear pre-loss photos
  • Receipts for dealer-installed accessories or aftermarket work

Check the configuration in value order

Start with the facts most likely to place the vehicle in a different shopping category: trim, body style, drivetrain, engine, transmission, battery or powertrain version, and major factory package. Then check individual features such as driver-assistance equipment, premium audio, seating configuration, towing package, wheels, roof, lighting, and interior material.

A long equipment list can still miss the important difference. A report may show heated seats and a sunroof while assigning the wrong drivetrain. Keep the correction list focused on facts that identify the vehicle or affect market value.

Separate three kinds of equipment

  1. Factory configuration: trim, packages, and options installed when built.
  2. Dealer-installed equipment: items added before or at sale.
  3. Aftermarket modifications: upgrades or changes made later.

These categories may be treated differently under the policy and in the used market. A factory package can affect which vehicles are proper comparables. An aftermarket part may require receipts, an endorsement, or evidence that buyers pay more for the complete vehicle. Cost is not the same as added market value.

Attach the few documents that prove the correction. Do not bury the strongest point beneath dozens of maintenance receipts.

Ask for the report to be rerun

If the trim or drivetrain is wrong, ask the insurer to correct the loss vehicle and issue a revised valuation. Then compare the old and new versions. Check whether the comparable pool, equipment adjustments, and final value changed. A corrected label with the same mismatched comps may not solve the problem.

Next, review the comparable vehicles and use the counteroffer worksheet to keep each request tied to evidence.
