The check is low. Find out why.

A low check does not always mean the vehicle value is low. It may reflect a deductible, loan payoff, or salvage deduction. Start with the gross settlement, then work backward until you find the line that needs evidence or an explanation.

By Mark West, Founder · Last reviewed July 21, 2026

1. The report describes the wrong vehicle

Wrong trim, drivetrain, engine, transmission, mileage, or factory package can corrupt the comparable search. Verify the configuration with the VIN, window sticker, build sheet, purchase records, and photos. Use the trim and options checklist.

2. The comparable cars are weak matches

Check model generation, body style, mechanical configuration, mileage, condition, seller, title history, location, listing date, and availability. A car can share a model name and still sit in a different market segment. See how to test each comparable.

3. A listing is stale, unavailable, or described incorrectly

Open the source listing and call the dealer when a material fact is unclear. Save the URL, capture date, VIN, price, and confirmation. A verifiable record is more useful than calling the listing fake.

4. Mileage moved the value the wrong way

Confirm both odometers and follow the direction of the adjustment. Then ask how the amount was derived. There is no universal rate that fits every vehicle. Use the mileage audit to work the line.

5. The condition line lacks support

Find the baseline, component ratings, observations, and calculation. Use dated pre-loss photos, tire receipts, service records, and inspection notes where they address the specific rating. Routine maintenance does not add its full cost to market value.

6. The report and local market answer different questions

Trade-in value, private-party asking price, dealer retail, book value, and policy-defined actual cash value are not interchangeable. Make sure your evidence matches the policy question. A high asking price alone does not prove what a vehicle would sell for.

7. Taxes or fees are missing from the gross settlement

Review the settlement statement, not just the valuation report. The treatment of taxes and fees depends on the policy, claim path, and state rules. Texas readers can use the sales-tax and settlement guide.

8. The deductible reduced the net payment

A deductible can lower the check without changing the car's value. Confirm whether the claim is under your own coverage, which deductible applies, and whether another insurer may later reimburse it through subrogation. Do not add the deductible to your ACV demand.

9. The lender received part or all of the payment

The insurer may name the lienholder on the payment or send funds directly to it. Compare the gross settlement with the loan payoff. Negative equity does not prove the car is worth more, and a high ACV does not erase every loan balance.

10. You elected to keep the salvage

When an owner keeps a totaled vehicle, the insurer may deduct the salvage value from the payment. Confirm the amount, title consequences, lender approval, repair economics, and future insurability before deciding.

Is a low offer automatically a scam?

No. It can be a correct offer, a correct value with a misunderstood deduction, a correctable data error, a debatable methodology, or a genuine dispute over the amount of loss. Unfair claim-handling allegations require facts and law beyond disappointment with the number. Start by requesting the complete report and documenting what can be checked.
